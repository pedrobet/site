A MANIFESTO

 

The digital transformations that continue to impact markets, institutions, businesses, governments, people and marketing now, after the pandemic, already allow us to envision more clearly the consequences of the acceleration of network learning from 2020-2022. While no technological breakthroughs emerged in the period, practice changed a lot: what was incipient or possible future became an unavoidable reality. The signs that we must move towards a new way of thinking about and doing marketing have ceased to be latent and become clear, some of them obvious. Some certainties - such as the use of technology and data in marketing - have become consensus and we should no longer consider them as components of the future of marketing, as they have quickly passed into present-day marketing.

The inevitable question, in the face of so many changes, many of which were held back since the arrival of the internet, social media, smartphones, then, is ... what should we take into account to build a new marketing, which is associated with and deals with the future of business?

How to think about and do new marketing, whose results go far beyond brand exposure and reach indicators associated with higher organizational performance, from increased transaction volume to profitability, from value creation to brand reputation and business innovation? Questions like these led us to analyze the future of marketing within the context of a new knowledge economy, based on observations about strategy, design, business, technology, innovation and, of course, what is already known about marketing.

To begin the conversation, we need to treat marketing as part of, and immersed in, the PHYGITAL world. This alone brings grand implications for the value creation process in a PHYSICAL environment, which is being enabled and extended by a DIGITAL dimension, the two orchestrated in the SOCIAL space, in [almost] real time. Campaigns should already be conceived, structured and executed in three dimensions - physical, social and digital - in a complementary and non-exclusive way.

It took us a long time to understand that digital is not just one more channel, as happened with the arrival of TV in the golden age of radio. The digital transformation changes the architectures and structures of marketing, which should have corresponded to a new way of thinking about strategy and the marketing execution process.

In the PHYGITAL world everything is much faster and the environment is not the same, stable for a long time. The digital and social dimensions of the space are written in code and are continuously rewritten by all the agents competing in the PHYGITAL world. Moreover, the internet has made each of us a medium; just look at digital influencers to see how this happened on a large scale. In such a context, can we still be sure that marketing campaigns or plans designed a year in advance have any chance of being effective?

Fast and radical changes in marketing were not only necessary during the pandemic, when we learned to change many things, almost everything, overnight. The same context has become a reality, with less instability, but with the fluidity of the digital and social dimensions and a look at the almost real time of marketing events and situations that should lead us to rethink what to do, with whom and for whom, and where to go [almost] every day.

And knowing where to go in contexts that change so quickly demands an understanding of strategy and, consequently, of marketing strategies.

Can the content, ads and influencers combo be considered, as many assume, the, or a, marketing strategy? Definitely not. A marketing strategy needs to solve strategic business problems and not just content creativity, development, positioning and media exposure.

What do you think of a gorgeous campaign, with huge exposure, but which has no product on the shelf to buy? This is a classic case. And whose problem is it? Trade? Sales? Marketing? Let's continue with more everyday scenes. The day starts with meetings, with various partners. On the agenda, approval of media plans, alignment of content that will go to social networks, event production and so on. Before that, did marketing participate in the product design stage? The offer? Do we assess whether what is being done adds value to the target? Did we define microsegmentation or do we still believe that the audience for a campaign is general? If we haven't thought about any of this yet, we reduce marketing to nothing more than a product promoter [or the P in promotion]. There, the marketing strategy did not exist!

Now, let's think of another scenario. Imagine that in a campaign to attract new students, the enrollment goal is not reached, no matter how much was invested in ads, leaflets, etc. To change the course of events, the marketing team discovers that the creation of a new course may be the key to a change in business strategy for that educational center. The marketing effort discovered, created, articulated and satisfied the need of a group. In this case, marketing assessed market supply and demand and designed the Product Market Fit [PMF].

Marketing left the reductionism of merely arousing attention to existing offers and incorporated 4 more logics - discovering, creating, articulating and satisfying - people's needs as customers. This is how marketing becomes an essential part of business strategy and its processes, not a collateral, only required to promote the offer when it is "ready" ... and maybe wrong.

Business strategy needs to transform people's aspirations, in the market, into market solutions that solve people's problems, in the market.

Understanding aspirations means understanding value creation, studying behaviors and habits and having the ability to assess whether products or services meet market demands and, if not, be part of the redesign process of the offer. It is marketing as strategy, it is marketing strategy.

Does this mean that we can only bring to market products that already have declared or identified aspirations? Of course not. We can also create needs. What we want to draw attention to here is that marketing strategy is part of the business model and should begin - and continue - to be thought of along with it. And, if we want to delve a little deeper, we need to know the difference between marketing strategy and strategic marketing.

Marketing strategy can be seen as the most abstract and general plan to reach and acquire customers, retain them and build the company's brand value. It involves everything from defining the target audience, product impacts and choosing how to reach the market, which may not use, for example, media.

Strategic marketing deals with the systematic processes of creating and executing a plan to reach a target already established by the business strategy [and marketing, of course] and, based on the techniques of connectivity, relationship, interaction, creation of meanings and formation of communities and communication [CRISC2] in the PHYGITAL world, achieve CRISC2 goals and not necessarily business.

But so far, you might say none of this is new. Because Plato already said, in the 4th century, that marketing is creating greater efficiency in market exchanges. Not only that: one of the best marketing texts, Marketing Myopia, by Theodore Levitt, published in the Harvard Business Review 63 years ago, states that marketing is one of the essences of the strategy of any business and that the role of marketing far transcends advertising [the promotion of some product and/or service to an audience or community] and propaganda [more or less systematic effort to manipulate people's actions, views and/or beliefs].

It doesn't stop there... and we won't go into the history and debates about marketing theories. But it is good to remember that the evolution of concepts, from the 4Ps (Product, Price, Place and Promotion) to the 4Cs (Customer, Cost, Convenience and Communication), to the 4As (Analysis, Adaptation, Activation and Evaluation) and even marketing 5.0 were not enough to break the organizational dynamics of departmentalizing marketing and placing it on the periphery of the business.

If we really want to build marketing for the future, the first step is to bring marketing strategy and the marketing function itself to the core of the business. And not to treat the core, in this context, as a hierarchical position, but as an articulating, orchestrating role across all business functions, making marketing a hub. In a network: in the network economy, networked businesses are networks. And in the network of sustainable businesses, business strategy merges with marketing strategy.

In more than one way, marketing as a business strategy hub spreads throughout the organization, like a virus, ceasing to be an isolated department and demanded as a "parallel organization", the destination for commercial demands, for example. Because it is not just by adding technology, data and agile methodologies to present-day marketing that we will build the marketing of the future.

The second step to designing and implementing the marketing of the future is to design strategic marketing to operate in a new knowledge economy, which is guided by a logic of PHYGITAL business platforms, which use information and communication technologies and data as subsidies to create connections, relationships and interactions - in the form of flows, programmable - for potentially exponential growth. We need to stop thinking about how to "convey" messages and start "creating dialogues" with our customers.

These two steps articulate marketing strategy and strategic marketing from, in and out of the business, in a network, with and for its network of partners, suppliers and customers.

What we have just described is the marketing transformation we are still experiencing today, whose ancestral roots no longer serve as the foundation for the future of marketing. The new marketing that is beginning to consolidate now accompanies the transformation of businesses that, soon, to survive, will be PHYGITAL. All of them. No exception. On PHYGITAL platforms. And if there was one thing that should have already happened, it is the transformation of marketing into business transformation.

PHYGITAL platforms have changed markets by introducing new competitive dynamics, very different from the traditional ones and, in many cases, displacing traditional companies, as we all know. Just remember AirBnb, Uber, Netflix, Nike and so on. Platforms enable ecosystems of simultaneous competition and cooperation, mediating the interaction between agents of all kinds and coordinating supply and demand on all sides of the market, connecting consumers, suppliers, complements and intermediaries.

Platforms facilitate connections, relationships, interactions and transactions between the multiple parties they serve, always trying to make agents on one side have greater incentives and likelihood of entering the platform as more members on the other sides do.

And making platforms popular is not a problem that is solved by traditional advertising campaigns, let alone by using digital as a channel. One of the marketing strategies is feeding the constant feedback cycle, for example, which leads, through the use of Artificial Intelligence, to the customization of the experience. There, the power of recommendation becomes crucial in marketing strategy. This is because platforms cause and at the same time demand network effects, which redraw a whole flow of connections, relationships, interactions and communication.

Imagining that a platform is starting from scratch, the first problem that network effects have to solve is the chicken-and-egg problem: how can the platform attract a minimum volume of users, in its initial stage, capable of making it evolve into an ecosystem? Or ... if there are not enough suppliers, how to bring customers so that there are and vice versa? Think of a strategy to popularize a marketplace and you will materialize the problem.

How should we attract store owners to showcase their products and people to buy at the same time? If we don't have a good mix of stores and products, we won't have interested people -customers- and if we don't have interested people we don't have the store owner's interest in showcasing their products. The classic way of [trying] to popularize digital platforms is by using the power of advertising [analog or digital], which [1] was never the appropriate mechanism to inhabit platform ecosystems, [2] shows clear signs of exhaustion and [3] has become unsustainable, from the point of view of advertising costs, for example, in relation to performance.

The trend in digital business is to create its own social network, to be its own search engine, creating its network effects, which are, in a very summarized way, strategies so that its product or service has so much engagement that its use grows exponentially. And this engagement only happens when your product or service becomes valuable to [all] others, attracting more and more users from increased buying and use by more users.

In this case, marketing creates nodes in the network, and seeks connections and routes between them: the more nodes [more agents, in communities] and more connections and routes between them, the better for each node and the entire network. And the strategies for this are often unique, requiring a sophisticated degree of personalization and micro-segmentation. Just remember the Orkut communities and how we liked to talk to people interested in the same topics as us, our bubbles.

According to the 2023 Consumer Behavior Trends survey, 76% of those connected get involved in online communities and of these, 81% increased their participation in groups during COVID19. This is one of the signs that humans continue to function as they always have: connected to each other, interdependent, gregarious, making alliances, looking for common points of association.

In the digital world, communities [re]design products, services, businesses and even organizations. A key role of marketing in the future is to create the connections that, further ahead, originate the common meanings that serve as the basis for the formation of communities where the brand is at the same time a producer, curator and recipient of content. But the connections must be anchored in a purpose, a belief that it is possible to create value for all its stakeholders, leveraging technology to solve complex problems and improve people's lives.

There is no room in the marketing of the future for brands without purpose on the world we live in. The Social dimension of the Digital world, which we talked about at the beginning of the text, will increasingly require such positioning. Our marketing plan needs to design and structure mechanisms for collaboration and belonging to business networks, creating specific agendas connected with the environmental, social, cultural, economic and political context.

Building the marketing of the future involves a radical change in marketing, a new structuring of it in organizations, incorporating business strategies into its composition and creating new narratives capable of connecting communities, against the backdrop of a significant exchange of old analog [or digitized analog] foundations for an environment where people are at the center of the business and redefine, all the time, not only the business, but the market. The marketing of the future is already being done now, it does not wait, and only those who dare, create and test new formats, leaving behind the old way of marketing, will know how to do it.

If we wanted to mark our proposition for the marketing of the future with a few letters and words ... we would start from the fact that marketing is a long word, in English, but with only three vowels ... in alphabetical order. Using the other vowels in the Latin alphabet, O and U, we could say that the keywords for the marketing of the future, its AEIOU, are ... Ambient, stratEgy, Interactions, Operations, Unification.

In the BUSINESS and marketing ENVIRONMENT, the facets of the business are orchestrated and treated as MARKETING STRATEGY and as part of strategic marketing, and should always lead to INTERACTIONS in ecosystem communities enabled by platforms. All marketing OPERATIONS, and the rest of the business, occur in the conjunction of dimensions of the PHYGITAL space, and can and should be UNIFIED with the strategy of the business as a whole, to achieve its purpose, goals and targets.

Of course, it is much more than that. But these are at least the starting points for the marketing of the future, for marketing in a state of flow, for marketing that is continuously redesigned, because it understands the digital space, where business already competes and will compete even more intensely, which is fluid, dynamic, and changes at a frequency that only increases and requires, from marketing and business, as a whole, an increasing agility and speed.


A manifesto for the marketing of the future
 

  1. Digital and social are not, have never been, channels. They are dimensions of the digital space, where flows of connections, relationships, interactions and creation of common meanings are initiated, evolve and, at some point, lead to transactions, themselves part of the great flow of business, the source of new connections, relationships ... ad infinitum.
     
  2. The future is networked. And the present already happens in networks. In networks, platforms and network effects enable communities, where markets become narratives, partly created by businesses, partly by people, groups, communities. In the network, every business is a narrative.
     
  3. Marketing in the future is from, to and on platforms. Platforms that enable ecosystems. Ecosystems of networked agents that form communities, markets. Networked agents who are recognized, rewarded and remunerated through incentives created by network effects.
     
  4. Marketing in the future has purpose. Because future businesses have purpose. Because communities and organizations should always emerge and evolve on commonly created meanings, communally created, in a network. And purpose, sustainable, cannot be of the organization, it must be of the ecosystem, shared by all agents in the network.
     
  5. Marketing strategy and strategic marketing are an essential part of business strategy. Also because they merge with it in almost everything that concerns communities, markets and ecosystems, where business lives and develops.
     
  6. The marketing of the future redesigns the architecture of the business. In physical markets, marketing's big problem in the past was finding customers for products. In digital markets, in the future, the problem of marketing is to find products for customers. In the present, marketing has little time to migrate from the past to the future.
     
  7. The marketing of the future changes the organization of the business. Digital marketing is at the heart of the business. But not a hierarchical center, much less a silo among many. In the digital world, businesses are networks, and the marketing of the future is a hub, through which all narratives of, about and for the business and its network flow and are affected.
     
  8. The marketing of the future happens in [almost] real time. In the digital world the physical dimension is extended by the digital, and the plane defined by the two is orchestrated in the social dimension, in near real time. In the future, there are no marketing strategies - and budgets - written in stone for the whole year. Not even for months. Nor strategic marketing actions that repeat as if the world were standing still. It isn't. The dynamics of the digital world require marketing with the same dynamics just to follow the leaders. To be a leader, you need to be more agile and faster. And start earlier.
     
  9. AEIOU: the actions of the marketing of the future are orchestrated in all facets of the business and of the business in its ambient in the markets. In the business and marketing environment, marketing stratEgy and stratEgic marketing are the foundations for creating interactions in communities in the ecosystems enabled by platforms for and from the business. All marketing operations must be unified in the phygital space with the operations and strategy of the business as a whole, to achieve its purpose, objectives and goals.
     
  10. The marketing of the future demands competent people. It is necessary to develop, at scale, the skills and abilities for digital marketing. The fundamentals of marketing of the future are still being established, and will be in a state of flux for a long time. The dynamics of human capital formation for the marketing of the future requires a combination of education [theory] and experience [practice] that needs to be developed by and for all organizations, now. Professionals in the marketing of the future need to be developed now. Today. And continuously evolve this process.

Introduction: The Imperative for a New Marketing Framework
in the Age of Phygital Platforms

 

The marketing landscape is undergoing a seismic shift, propelled by digital technologies, the proliferation of data, and the emergence of networked societies. Manuel Castells' seminal work on the network society (Castells, M. "The Rise of the Network Society," Blackwell, 1996) provides a theoretical underpinning for understanding the fluidity and interconnectedness of modern social structures. We aim to extend Castells' theory of flows to conceptualize a future-oriented marketing theory, encapsulated in the AEIOU framework.

The Manifesto for the Marketing of the Future, which serves as the foundational text for this discourse, posits that traditional marketing paradigms are increasingly inadequate for navigating the complexities of modern markets. These markets are not merely digital or physical; they are phygital, a neologism that captures the convergence of physical, digital, and social dimensions. The manifesto argues that the future of marketing lies in understanding and leveraging this phygital nexus, facilitated by platforms and enriched by network effects.

The need for a new framework becomes even more pressing when we consider the role of platforms in shaping modern markets. Platforms, as defined by Parker, Van Alstyne, and Choudary in "Platform Revolution" (Parker, G., Van Alstyne, M., & Choudary, S.P. "Platform Revolution," Norton & Company, 2016), are not mere digital interfaces but are complex ecosystems that facilitate interactions between multiple stakeholders. These interactions are not linear but are characterized by network effects, where the value of the platform increases with the number of participants. This creates a self-reinforcing loop of value creation and capture, fundamentally altering the dynamics of competition and cooperation.

Moreover, the manifesto underscores the role of communities in these phygital platforms. Communities are not passive recipients of value but active participants in value creation. This aligns with the concept of "prosumers," a term coined by Alvin Toffler in "The Third Wave" (Toffler, A. "The Third Wave," William Morrow and Company, 1980), which refers to consumers who are also producers. In phygital platforms, communities co-create value, thereby blurring the traditional boundaries between producers and consumers.

The role of data in these phygital ecosystems cannot be overstated. Data is not merely a byproduct of transactions but is a critical asset that drives decision-making and strategy. This is in line with the manifesto's emphasis on the importance of data-driven decision-making in the phygital age. The rise of Big Data and analytics has given organizations unprecedented insights into consumer behavior, but it has also raised ethical and regulatory concerns, as highlighted by the Cambridge Analytica scandal and the subsequent discussions around data privacy and governance.

Furthermore, the manifesto draws attention to the "flows" of connections, relations, interactions, and narratives that permeate these phygital platforms. This is a direct extension of Manuel Castells' theory of flows, which posits that in a network society, power and influence are decentralized and are distributed across complex networks of social relations. Marketing strategies, therefore, must be designed to tap into these flows, facilitating interactions and co-creating value rather than merely pushing products or services.

The AEIOU framework, which serves as the cornerstone of this paper, is an attempt to synthesize these complex, interrelated phenomena into a coherent, actionable strategy for the marketing of the future. It is not merely an academic exercise but is grounded in the practical realities and challenges of marketing in the phygital age. Each component of the AEIOU framework—Ambient, stratEgy, Interactions, Operations, and Unification—addresses a critical dimension of this new marketing landscape, offering a multi-layered approach to understanding and navigating the complexities of phygital platforms.

In summary, the confluence of digital technologies, data proliferation, and network effects has created a new marketing landscape that is complex, dynamic, and fluid. Traditional marketing paradigms, rooted in the industrial age, are ill-equipped to navigate this new reality. The manifesto for the marketing of the future serves as a clarion call for a new framework, one that is attuned to the nuances and complexities of phygital platforms. The AEIOU framework, which we aim to elaborate in this paper, offers a robust, comprehensive approach to understanding and implementing marketing strategies for this new age. It encapsulates the multi-dimensionality of modern markets and provides actionable insights for organizations seeking to thrive in this brave new world.

 


Ambient (A): The Competitive, Phygital, Networked Environment
in the Age of Platforms

The term "Ambient" encapsulates the milieu in which modern organizations operate—a complex, networked environment that is simultaneously physical, digital, and social. The manifesto for the marketing of the future posits that the Ambient is not merely a backdrop but a dynamic, interactive space that shapes and is shaped by organizational strategies. It argues that the traditional marketing function, often siloed and isolated from other organizational functions, is ill-suited for this new reality. Marketing must evolve from a department to a cross-functional capability, integrated into the very fabric of the organization. This section aims to delve into the intricacies of the Ambient, focusing on the key actions of Design, Develop, and Deliver.

We need a new approach to understand the competitive ambient.

The manifesto emphasizes that the Ambient is not a static entity but a complex, evolving ecosystem. Traditional marketing theories, rooted in the 4Ps (Product, Price, Place, Promotion), are increasingly inadequate for capturing the nuances of this ecosystem. The Ambient is characterized by network effects, where the value of the ecosystem increases with the number of participants. This is in line with Metcalfe's Law, which states that the value of a network is proportional to the square of the number of connected users (Metcalfe, R. "Metcalfe's Law: A Network Becomes More Valuable as It Reaches More Users," InfoWorld, 1995).

Moreover, the manifesto argues that competitiveness in this Ambient is not merely a function of price or quality but involves creating value within the ecosystem. This aligns with the Resource-Based View of the firm, which posits that sustainable competitive advantage is achieved by leveraging unique resources and capabilities (Barney, J. "Firm Resources and Sustained Competitive Advantage," Journal of Management, 1991).

The demise of marketing as an isolated department.

The manifesto contends that the marketing function can no longer operate in isolation, given the interconnected nature of the Ambient. This is consistent with the concept of "boundary-spanning roles," where marketing professionals must engage with multiple stakeholders, both internal and external, to create value (Aldrich, H. & Herker, D. "Boundary Spanning Roles and Organization Structure," Academy of Management Review, 1977). The manifesto calls for a reimagining of the marketing function, transforming it from a department to a cross-functional capability that permeates the entire organization.

Design: Marketing as the Orchestrator of Scenario Drafting in the Networked Market

Organizations must design scenarios that account for the multifaceted nature of networked markets. Scenario planning, a technique rooted in game theory, allows firms to anticipate various market conditions (Schoemaker, P.J.H. "Scenario Planning: A Tool for Strategic Thinking," Sloan Management Review, 1995).

In the AEIOU framework, marketing emerges not merely as a function but as the central orchestrator in defining the competitive landscape. The manifesto for the marketing of the future posits that marketing's role must evolve from a siloed department to a cross-functional leader, adept at leveraging modern design approaches for scenario planning. This transformation is not just a strategic necessity but an imperative for survival and competitiveness in the networked market.

Marketing and the Lean Startup Methodology

In this reimagined role, marketing takes the lead in applying the Lean Startup methodology to design minimal viable scenarios (MVS). Here, marketing acts as the conduit between customer feedback and organizational strategy, ensuring that the scenarios are not only viable but also aligned with customer needs and market demands. The iterative nature of the Lean Startup methodology allows marketing to continuously refine these scenarios, making them more adaptive to the ever-changing Ambient.

Game Theoretical Models and Marketing Strategy

Marketing also assumes a pivotal role in employing game theoretical models to anticipate the strategies of various market stakeholders. By doing so, marketing can craft strategies that are not just reactive but proactive, positioning the organization favorably in various possible future states of the market. This strategic foresight is invaluable in a networked market where competitive dynamics are fluid and often unpredictable.

Agent-Based Simulation: Marketing as the Choreographer

In the application of Agent-Based Simulation, marketing functions as the choreographer of a complex dance involving multiple stakeholders. By simulating the behavior of these stakeholders, marketing gains insights into emergent phenomena like network effects, which are critical in the networked market. These insights inform not just marketing strategies but also product development, customer engagement, and even regulatory compliance.

Data-Driven Predictive Models: Marketing as the Data Strategist

Incorporating data-driven predictive models, marketing transitions into the role of a data strategist. By validating or refining scenarios through real-world data, marketing ensures that organizational strategies are not just theoretically sound but empirically validated. This data-centric approach allows marketing to make more informed and timely decisions, reducing market uncertainty.

Conclusion: Marketing as the Central Orchestrator in the Ambient Stage of AEIOU

In sum, marketing assumes a core, orchestrating role in the Ambient, integrating modern methodologies like the Lean Startup, Game Theoretical Models, Agent-Based Simulation, Data-Driven Predictive Models and as many others as needed into the design phase of scenario planning. This integrated approach aligns perfectly with the manifesto's call for a more agile, cross-functional approach to marketing. Marketing becomes the linchpin that connects various organizational functions, guiding them through the complexities of the Ambient. It is not just about promoting products or building brand equity; it is about defining the very scenarios in which the organization, its products, services, and solutions will compete. By assuming this central role, marketing becomes an indispensable strategic function, deeply embedded in the organization's overall strategy and operations, and crucial for navigating the intricacies of the networked market.

Develop: Marketing as the Architect of Networked Markets

Top of FormThe development phase involves creating or approaching networked markets. This is facilitated by leveraging platform ecosystems, a concept that has gained traction in recent years (Parker, G., Van Alstyne, M., & Choudary, S.P. "Platform Revolution," Norton & Company, 2016).

The "Develop" stage within the Ambient facet of the AEIOU framework is a crucible for the initial and subsequent approaches to networked markets. This is not a one-off endeavor but a continuum, sometimes involving the creation of new markets and at other times, strategic entry into existing ones. The manifesto for the future of marketing underscores the imperative of this dynamic, multi-faceted approach, positioning marketing not merely as a peripheral function but as the central orchestrator in this complex landscape.

Marketing's Role in Platform Ecosystems

In the context of Ambient, the platform ecosystem serves as the foundational infrastructure for networked markets. Whether the platform is proprietary or a third-party entity, marketing's role is to architect the value proposition and the interaction mechanisms that will attract and retain participants. Drawing from the manifesto's emphasis on phygital platforms, marketing must ensure that these platforms seamlessly integrate physical, digital, and social dimensions, thereby creating a holistic user experience.

Community Building and Network Effects

The second pillar in this development stage is community building, which is intrinsically tied to network effects. Marketing must not only attract initial users but also cultivate a sense of community that will trigger network effects, thereby amplifying the value of the platform. This involves a deep understanding of social dynamics, behavioral economics, and the unique value that the platform offers to different stakeholders. The manifesto highlights the importance of communities as facilitators of network effects, and marketing must leverage this to either create or penetrate networked markets effectively.

Strategic Market Entry and Competitive Positioning

When the focus is on entering an existing networked market, marketing must employ game-theoretical models to analyze competitive dynamics and identify optimal entry strategies. This could involve forming alliances, leveraging existing communities, or even disrupting current value chains. The manifesto calls for a strategic, data-driven approach, and marketing must rise to this challenge by employing predictive analytics and real-time data to make informed decisions.

Data-Driven Decision Making and Feedback Mechanisms

In both scenarios—whether creating a new market or entering an existing one—data-driven decision-making is crucial. Marketing must establish robust feedback mechanisms that continually refine the platform and community strategies based on real-time data. This is not merely a tactical requirement but a strategic imperative, as emphasized in the manifesto. The feedback loops serve as the organization's sensory organs, providing the insights needed for iterative development and long-term sustainability.

Conclusion: Marketing as the Orchestrator in the Ambient's Develop Stage

In summary, the Develop stage within the Ambient framework is a complex, multi-dimensional endeavor that positions marketing as the orchestrator of the organization's initial and ongoing approaches to networked markets. By architecting platform ecosystems, building communities to trigger network effects, strategically entering markets, and employing data-driven feedback mechanisms, marketing serves as the linchpin that holds together the various elements of the Ambient framework. It is in this orchestrator role that marketing transcends its traditional boundaries to become a strategic function that not only communicates but also shapes the very architecture and dynamics of networked markets. This aligns perfectly with the manifesto's vision, making marketing not just a function but the central nervous system in the anatomy of future-oriented organizations.

Deliver: Ensuring Competitiveness Through Value Creation
in Networked Markets within the Ambient Framework

The Epistemological Foundations: Narratives as Cognitive Frameworks

Narratives are not mere stories; they are cognitive structures that shape our perception of reality and our understanding of the world. Drawing from the work of narrative theorists like Hayden White and Paul Ricoeur, we can understand that narratives serve as interpretive lenses, providing coherence and meaning to disparate events and experiences. In the context of marketing, this means that narratives can serve as powerful tools for shaping consumer perceptions and behaviors. They are not merely descriptive but prescriptive, influencing how we interpret and respond to events. In the realm of marketing, this epistemological function of narratives is particularly potent. For instance, a well-crafted brand narrative can shape consumer perceptions, not just about a product or service, but about entire lifestyles or worldviews. This aligns with the work of narrative theorists like Jerome Bruner, who posits that narratives create "reality" as much as they represent it.

The cognitive function of narratives is rooted in schema theory, which posits that our cognitive system organizes information into mental structures or "schemas." Narratives serve as complex schemas, providing a structured framework for interpreting new information. This is particularly relevant in the context of digital marketing, where consumers are inundated with information. A compelling narrative can serve as a cognitive anchor, helping consumers navigate the information overload.

The ethical dimensions of narrative strategies cannot be overstated. Narratives have the power to shape social norms and values, and therefore come with ethical responsibilities. This is particularly relevant in the age of "fake news" and misinformation. A narrative strategy that prioritizes ethical considerations is not just socially responsible but also more sustainable in the long term.

The ethical imperative in narrative strategies can be understood through the lens of "discourse ethics," a philosophical framework developed by Jürgen Habermas. Discourse ethics posits that ethical validity arises from the process of rational discourse and mutual understanding. In the context of marketing, this means that ethical narratives should be developed through a process of stakeholder engagement and dialogue, rather than top-down imposition. Narratives have the power to both enlighten and deceive. The Cambridge Analytica scandal serves as a cautionary tale of how narrative manipulation can have far-reaching social and political consequences. Therefore, any narrative strategy must be grounded in ethical principles, such as transparency, authenticity, and social responsibility.

  1. Narrative Identification and Mapping
    • Objective: To identify prevailing consumer narratives and map them onto different segments of the target audience.

The objective is to understand not just what consumers are saying, but why they are saying it. The methodological approach for narrative identification is rooted in grounded theory, a research methodology that involves the collection and analysis of qualitative data to develop theories. Grounded theory provides a systematic framework for identifying and categorizing consumer narratives, making it a valuable tool for narrative identification.
    • Tools: Social listening tools, sentiment analysis, and AI-driven community, people and consumer behavior analytics.
  2. Narrative Design, Prototyping, Testing and Validation
    • Objective: To construct a flexible brand narrative that aligns with organizational values and can adapt to changing consumer sentiments.
    • Before rolling out a new narrative, it's crucial to test its effectiveness. This involves both qualitative and quantitative methods, such as A/B testing, eye-tracking studies, and consumer surveys. The objective is to validate whether the narrative achieves its intended psychological and behavioral outcomes. The testing and validation phase is informed by the scientific method, involving hypothesis testing, data collection, and statistical analysis. This empirical approach ensures that the narrative is not just theoretically sound but also empirically validated.
    • Tools: Content management systems, AI-driven copywriting tools, and brand voice analyzers.
  3. Narrative Implementation, Dissemination and Amplification
    • Objective: To disseminate the brand narrative across multiple ecosystems of communities and amplify its reach through targeted marketing campaigns.
    • Once existing consumer narratives have been identified and mapped, the next step is to design new narratives that align with the brand's strategic objectives. This involves a multi-disciplinary approach that integrates insights from psychology, sociology, and semiotics to create narratives that are not only compelling but also culturally and psychologically resonant. From aligning the core message with strategic objectives to ensuring cultural sensitivity and ethical compliance, each point in the methodology serves as a critical building block in the construction of a compelling, effective, and responsible narrative. This is not merely an academic exercise but a practical guide that addresses the multi-faceted challenges and opportunities that characterize our networked world in the age of Artificial Intelligence and Big Data.

  1. Core Message Identification: The first step in crafting a narrative is to identify its core message, which should be in alignment with the objectives of your CRISC (Cyber Risk and Internal Control) framework. This is not merely a thematic focus but a strategic alignment that serves as the narrative's backbone. For instance, if your CRISC framework aims to enhance cybersecurity, your narrative could focus on the importance of data protection in the digital age (Schneier, "Data and Goliath," 2015).
     
  2. Archetypal Resonance: Drawing from Carl Jung's work on archetypes ("The Archetypes and the Collective Unconscious," 1959), create characters or scenarios that resonate emotionally with your audience. These archetypes serve as universal symbols that can be adapted to fit various narrative structures, from the Hero's Journey to the Transformation Arc (Campbell, "The Hero with a Thousand Faces," 1949).
     
  3. Vonnegut's Story Shapes: Kurt Vonnegut's theory on the shapes of stories provides a framework for crafting emotionally diverse narrative arcs. By plotting the protagonist's fortunes over time, Vonnegut's shapes offer a variety of emotional experiences, from tragedy to triumph ("Kurt Vonnegut on the Shapes of Stories," 2012).
     
  4. Behavioral Economics Integration: Incorporate principles from behavioral economics, such as "nudge theory," to subtly influence consumer behavior (Thaler & Sunstein, "Nudge," 2008). This allows you to craft narratives that not only engage but also guide the audience toward decisions.
     
  5. Data Life cycle and Analytics: Strategic management of organizational and communities’ data and the deployment of analytics tools like sentiment analysis and Natural Language Processing (NLP) to empirically validate your narrative (Liu, "Sentiment Analysis and Opinion Mining," 2012) is a sine qua non. This ensures that the narrative is not only emotionally compelling but also grounded in factual data.
     
  6. Empathy Creation: Leverage storytelling techniques to create empathy, drawing from Jerome Bruner's work on narrative cognition ("Acts of Meaning," 1990). This could involve first-person narratives or stories that highlight the human impact of your product or service.
     
  7. Multimedia Integration: Incorporate multimedia elements such as Augmented Reality (AR) or Virtual Reality (VR) to create an immersive narrative experience (Milgram & Kishino, "A Taxonomy of Mixed Reality Visual Displays," 1994). This enhances engagement and allows for a more interactive user experience.
     
  8. AI-Powered Personalization: Natural Language Generation (NLG) algorithms to personalize narratives based on individual user data (Gatt & Krahmer, "Survey of the State of the Art in Natural Language Generation," 2018) will be commonplace very soon, enabling a more targeted and personalized narrative experience.
     
  9. Ethical Review: Conduct a comprehensive ethical review, especially when AI is involved. This should include considerations around data privacy, algorithmic bias, and the potential for misinformation (O'Neil, "Weapons of Math Destruction," 2016).
     
  10. Impact Measurement: Employ Key Performance Indicators (KPIs) to measure the narrative's impact. This could range from engagement metrics to conversion rates, providing a quantitative assessment of the narrative's effectiveness (Parmenter, "Key Performance Indicators," 2015).
     
  11. Cultural Sensitivity: Ensure that the narrative is culturally sensitive and inclusive, taking into account the diverse backgrounds and perspectives of your audience (Hofstede, "Culture's Consequences," 1980).
     
  12. Legal Compliance: Ensure compliance with legal standards, especially concerning data usage and intellectual property rights (Lessig, "Code and Other Laws of Cyberspace," 1999).
     
  13. Feedback Loops: Establish mechanisms for real-time audience feedback, allowing for immediate adjustments and refinements to the narrative (Eric Ries, "The Lean Startup," 2011).
     
  14. Iterative Refinement: Adopt an iterative approach to narrative crafting, continuously refining based on empirical data and audience feedback. This aligns with the Agile methodology, emphasizing adaptability and responsiveness (Beck et al., "Agile Manifesto," 2001).
     
  15. Scalability: Ensure that the narrative can be scaled across different platforms and media, maintaining its core message and emotional resonance (Reeves & Nass, "The Media Equation," 1996).
     
  16. Accessibility: Make the narrative accessible to people with disabilities, adhering to the Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG) for digital platforms (Henry et al., "Just Ask: Integrating Accessibility Throughout Design," 2007).
     
  17. {Regional, Global}ization: Adapt the narrative for different languages and cultures, ensuring that it resonates universally while also catering to local sensibilities (Ghemawat, "World 3.0," 2011). Remember theat places like Brazil are not the “same people and habits everywhere”.
     
  18. Historical Context: Provide the historical context where necessary, not only adding depth and richness to the narrative, but avoiding many pitfalls that would make it work against you instataneously (Fischer, "Historians' Fallacies," 1970).
     
  19. Future Projections: Integrate future trends and projections into the narrative, making it forward-looking and relevant in a rapidly changing landscape (Schwartz, "The Art of the Long View," 1991).
     
  20. Narrative Coherence: Ensure that the narrative is coherent and logically structured, providing a seamless user experience. This involves maintaining a consistent tone, style, and thematic focus throughout the narrative (Genette, "Narrative Discourse," 1980).

By meticulously integrating these twenty points into your narrative crafting process, you can create compelling, effective narratives that not only engage your audience but also drive strategic objectives. Designing and Developing Business Processes in Networked Environments:
A Paradigm Shift from Centralized BPM

Introduction

The acceleration of digital transformation is catalyzing a fundamental reconfiguration of business processes and architectures. Traditional business process management (BPM) methodologies with their emphasis on centralized control, standardized workflows and enterprise-wide optimization struggle to keep pace with the speed, complexity and unpredictability of today's business environment (Vom Brocke et al., 2016).

In response, a new paradigm is emerging focused on designing and developing Adaptive Process Networks (AProN). Unlike traditional BPM, this approach recognizes that value creation increasingly occurs through decentralized orchestration within and across organizational ecosystems (Ølnes et al., 2017). AProN embraces principles of distribution, decentralization, asynchronous interactions and protocols to create an agile, resilient and adaptable business architecture.

Foundations of designing and developing Adaptive Process Networks

Designing and developing Adaptive Process Networks draws foundational principles from management cybernetics (Beer, 1984), platform economics (Parker et al., 2016), distributed organizational models (Puranam et al., 2014) and complexity leadership theory (Uhl-Bien et al., 2007). It reconceptualizes the organization not as a centralized authority but as a networked ecosystem coevolving with participants through context-specific interactions, information flows and collective sensemaking (Stacey, 2011; Merali, 2006).

This aligns with the shift from firm-centric to human-centric paradigms in the digital era, where value is co-created through relationships, shared purpose and empowered engagement (Merchant, 2022). Traditional BPM principles of control, structure and optimization give way to responsiveness, adaptability and generative emergence (De Smet & Gagnon, 2018).

Four Pillars of Adaptive Process Networks (AProN)

The foundations of designing and developing AProN draw from diverse theoretical frameworks that provide complementary perspectives on decentralized and emergent coordination.

Management Cybernetics: Stafford Beer's Viable System Model (Beer, 1984) within management cybernetics views organizations as adaptable systems balancing stability and change. It elucidates how autonomous, distributed systems can self-organize in alignment with collective purpose through mechanisms like homeostasis and recursion. This insight into decentralized coordination and contextual adaptation informs designing and developing Adaptive Process Networks.

Platform Economics: Parker, Van Alstyne and Choudary's theory of platforms revolutionizing business through matchmaking and network effects (Parker et al., 2016) reveals architectural principles for leveraging interfaces, incentives and governance for scalable ecosystem participation. AProN adapts these platform design levers to coordinate distributed processes.

Distributed Organizations: Puranam, Alexy and Reitzig's research on distributed organizations replaces authority with accountability through greater transparency (Puranam et al., 2014). By rendering activities and contributions visible across the network, participants align with shared goals. AProN draws from this autonomous, networked paradigm.

Complexity Leadership: Uhl-Bien, Russ and McKelvey's complexity leadership theory (Uhl-Bien et al., 2007) describes how distributed control, collective learning and network intelligence enable organizing under uncertainty. AProN leverages these generative leadership principles to spur innovation and adaptability.

Sensemaking in Networks: Merali's synthesis of complexity and sensemaking (Merali, 2006) and Stacey's work on strategic choice in business ecosystems (Stacey, 2011) provide theoretical grounding for how shared meaning and narrative emerge from local interactions to shape collective reality and identities in decentralized networks. AProN thus moves from process structure to sensegiving.

Human-Centric Paradigms: Merchant's writing on the human enterprise (Merchant, 2022) encapsulates the broader paradigm shift from mechanistic to humanistic paradigms where organizing logics align with human values like purpose, autonomy and self-actualization. AProN adopts this human-centered ethos rather than transactional efficiency.

In summary, AProN integrates cybernetic, platform, organizational, leadership, sensemaking and humanistic perspectives to reconceptualize business processes as shifting from optimization to ongoing adaptation through local empowerment, distribution, emergence and collective sensegiving. This synthesis provides a rich theoretical foundation for the principles and practices of AProN outlined in the article.

AProN rests on four foundational pillars adapted to the networked context:

  1. Decentralization is a guiding principle whereby decision autonomy resides at the nodes rather than a central controlling authority (Puranam et al., 2014). Local units leverage real-time information and collective objectives to take context-specific actions without bureaucratic oversight. This enables agile responses and distributed experimentation critical for digital era competitiveness.
     
  2. Distribution: Processes are designed as distributed systems across organizational units and external partners rather than monolithic structures (Ølnes et al., 2017). Modular architectures allow discrete activities to be parallelized across nodes for efficiency gains. Information is open and accessible rather than siloed and proprietary.
     
  3. Asynchronicity: Traditional processes often require synchronous interactions, leading to bottlenecks and delays. In AProN processes can proceed independently, waiting for the necessary inputs or triggers to continue. Interactions adopt asynchronous and event-driven models allowing processes to proceed independently rather than wait on synchronous inputs or approvals (De Smet & Gagnon, 2018). Events, triggers and protocols coordinate workflows. This fluidity increases adaptability to contextual variety and market dynamism.
     
  4. Networked Interactions: Business processes are not isolated but interconnected as complex adaptive systems exhibiting nonlinear dynamics and emergent behaviors (Stacey, 2011). Network science tools like graph theory and agent-based modeling describe interaction patterns, interdependencies and collective behaviors to enhance ecosystem resilience and orchestration. In AProN, processes are not isolated but interact with each other in complex ways, governed by network protocols [and , maybe, smart contracts], ensuring coherence and consistency across the network.

Architecting and Implementing AProN

Realizing AProN requires reimagining process architectures and implementation capabilities:

Modular Design: Processes are decomposed into self-contained subsystems with standardized interfaces enabling loose coupling and reusability. Holism arises from architectural integration rather than process consolidation (Schilling, 2000).

Platforms and APIs: Distributed processes require integrating platforms, shared services and open APIs allowing interoperability. Smart contracts execute based on codified protocols and decentralized consensus between participants (Reyna et al., 2018).

Data and Analytics: Data accumulation, analytics and collective sensemaking replace centralized oversight for coherence. Node behaviors adapt based on system-level insights from mining interaction patterns and emergent signals in real-time data.

Human Competencies: Skills in creative sensemaking, contextual decision-making, communicator-connector roles and social technologies facilitate human flourishing in decentralized networks (Merchant, 2022). Psychological safety and growth mindsets foster innovation.

Incentives Alignment: Shared goals and incentives promote collaboration rather than parochial interests. Tokenization aligns participants to collective value creation by mapping contributions to ecosystem tokens as rewards (Schmidt & Wagner, 2019).

Enabling Governance: Governance in a networked setting goes beyond traditional data governance to include platform governance. It involves setting the rules and protocols for interaction among the various nodes in the network. This governance ensures that despite decentralization and distribution, there is a coherent strategy and alignment with the overall business objectives. Culture and ethics provide behavioral guardrails in conditions of radical freedom.

Adapting Strategy: Strategizing moves from centralized planning to continuously sensing market signals and adjusting objectives, resource allocations and ecosystem positioning accordingly (Srivardhana & Pawlowski, 2007).

Conclusion

The development phase in a networked setting is a radical departure from traditional BPM and represents a paradigm shift in how we think about processes in marketing and business as a whole. By adopting a decentralized, distributed, and asynchronous approach, businesses can create a more agile and responsive strategy that is better aligned with the complexities of modern, networked environments.

AProN represents a paradigm attuned to the digital era where value creation and capture occur through human-centric, decentralized orchestration. It balances coherence with autonomy, resilience with adaptability and purpose with dynamism. As business complexity and clockspeed accelerate, AProN offers guiding principles for architecting an agile, innovative and collaborative enterprise in harmony with the future.

As Peter Drucker aptly stated, "The best way to predict the future is to create it." The development phase in a networked setting is where this creation happens, setting the stage for a more agile, responsive, and effective marketing strategy.
